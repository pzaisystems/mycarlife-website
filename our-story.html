From Parking to Thriving

How a rejected flashlight app evolved into a comprehensive framework for vehicle dwelling success.

Chapter 1: The Rejected Flashlight

It started with a simple idea: a flashlight app for people living in vehicles.

In late 2025, Padrick Zulueta — a full-time vehicle dweller living in a 2005 Toyota 4Runner while working as a delivery driver — built "Dim Light." The concept was straightforward: four colored lights in a diamond formation, with brightness controls optimized for stealth. Unlike a regular flashlight that blasts white light and draws attention, Dim Light started at 1% brightness. Perfect for 3 AM when you need to grab a water bottle without waking yourself up or alerting anyone outside.

He priced it at $2.99 and submitted it to Apple.

Apple rejected it as spam.

The rejection notice was blunt: "Your app prominently features flashlight functionality. There are already enough of these apps on the App Store."

But Apple's own rejection contained a hint: "Your app may include features or characteristics that distinguish it as more than just a flashlight app."

Padrick took that hint seriously.

Chapter 2: The Question That Changed Everything

The app needed a soul — a core concept that tied everything together. That's when the scoring system emerged.

The question was simple: How do you know if a parking spot is good?

Not "is it legal" or "is it safe" — but comprehensively good. The kind of spot where you can actually sleep well, feel secure, and not worry about a knock on the window at 3 AM.

Through research into vehicle dwelling communities, a framework emerged. Successful vehicle dwelling came down to four factors:

  • Visibility — Will I be noticed?
  • Legitimacy — Am I allowed here?
  • Safety — Is this spot secure?
  • Livability — Can I actually live here?

This became the foundation for the Spot Check — a quick assessment any vehicle dweller could run on any parking location.

Chapter 3: Surviving vs. Thriving

But something nagged at Padrick.

The Four Pillars measured whether you could park somewhere successfully. They said nothing about whether you were actually thriving at vehicle dwelling.

Someone could score 95% on all four pillars — invisible, allowed, safe spot — and still be eating garbage, never exercising, isolated from everyone, depressed and scrolling TikTok 14 hours a day, broke with no plan.

That's not thriving. That's surviving in a good parking spot.

The question evolved from "Is this spot good?" to "How well do you dwell?"

Chapter 4: The Seven Pillars

The Dwell Score expanded to measure the whole person, not just parking:

Vehicle Health 20%

Vehicle failure = lose shelter + transportation + employment simultaneously

Foundation 20%

Legal standing, documents, domicile — can't access jobs, benefits, or housing without valid ID

Resources 20%

Income, savings, financial sustainability — no money = can't fix problems in any other pillar

Wellbeing 15%

Physical health, mental state, sleep, nutrition, hygiene

Connection 10%

Relationships, community — isolation increases mortality risk

Purpose 10%

Direction, mindset — carries you through setbacks

Parking Stability 5%

Important but most recoverable — 30 minutes to find a new spot

From Spam to System

What started as a "$2.99 flashlight spam" rejection became something much more significant: a comprehensive framework for understanding what makes vehicle dwelling sustainable.

The Dwell Score isn't just a feature — it's the soul of the app. It represents a genuine attempt to understand what vehicle dwellers actually need, built collaboratively through questioning, research, and real-world insight.

Apple wanted to see something that wasn't just another flashlight. They got a framework that might actually help 3.1 million Americans understand whether they're surviving or thriving — and what they can do about it.

"How well do you dwell?"
